What Is Compound Interest?

Last updated: February 2026

In This Article

  1. Definition
  2. How It Works
  3. Key Components
  4. Common Misconceptions
  5. In Practice

Definition

Compound interest is a type of interest that is calculated on both the principal amount and any accrued interest, first described by Richard Price in 1769.

How It Works

Compound interest works by adding the interest to the principal amount at regular intervals, such as monthly or annually, which then becomes the new principal for the next period. This process creates a snowball effect, where the interest earned in each period is greater than the previous period, resulting in exponential growth. For instance, Albert Einstein famously described compound interest as "the eighth wonder of the world" due to its powerful effect on savings and investments. The frequency of compounding also affects the total amount of interest earned, with more frequent compounding resulting in higher earnings, as seen in the time value of money framework.

The power of compound interest is evident in the way it can transform small, regular investments into substantial sums over time. Benjamin Graham, a renowned investor, emphasized the importance of long-term investing and the role of compound interest in generating wealth. The rule of 72, a formula for estimating the number of years it takes for an investment to double in value based on the interest rate, is a useful tool for understanding the impact of compound interest. For example, an investment earning 8% interest will double in value approximately every 9 years, as calculated using the rule of 72 (72 / 8 = 9).

The impact of compound interest is also influenced by the inflation rate, which can erode the purchasing power of savings and investments over time. Milton Friedman's theory of monetary economics highlights the importance of considering inflation when making investment decisions. In the United States, for example, the average annual inflation rate has been around 2.5% (Bureau of Labor Statistics), which can significantly affect the purchasing power of savings and investments. By understanding the interplay between compound interest, inflation, and the time value of money, investors can make informed decisions and create effective long-term investment strategies.

Key Components

  • Principal amount: The initial amount of money invested or borrowed, which serves as the base for calculating interest. An increase in the principal amount will result in higher interest earnings, while a decrease will lead to lower earnings.
  • Interest rate: The percentage at which interest is calculated on the principal amount, which can be fixed or variable. An increase in the interest rate will result in higher interest earnings, while a decrease will lead to lower earnings.
  • Compounding frequency: The number of times interest is added to the principal amount per year, which can range from daily to annually. More frequent compounding will result in higher interest earnings, while less frequent compounding will lead to lower earnings.
  • Time: The duration for which the interest is calculated, which can be months, years, or decades. A longer time period will result in higher interest earnings, while a shorter time period will lead to lower earnings.
  • Inflation rate: The rate at which prices for goods and services are increasing, which can erode the purchasing power of savings and investments. A higher inflation rate will reduce the purchasing power of interest earnings, while a lower inflation rate will preserve it.
  • Risk tolerance: The investor's ability to withstand fluctuations in the value of their investment, which can affect their willingness to take on more risk in pursuit of higher returns. A higher risk tolerance will allow investors to pursue more aggressive investment strategies, while a lower risk tolerance will lead to more conservative approaches.

Common Misconceptions

Myth: Compound interest is only relevant for large investments — Fact: Even small, regular investments can benefit from compound interest, as seen in the dollar-cost averaging strategy.

Myth: Inflation always hurts investments — Fact: Some investments, such as Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS), are designed to protect against inflation and can even benefit from it.

Myth: Compound interest is a guaranteed way to get rich — Fact: Compound interest is a powerful tool, but it is not a guarantee of wealth, as Warren Buffett's investment philosophy emphasizes the importance of patience, discipline, and a well-thought-out investment strategy.

In Practice

The power of compound interest can be seen in the investment strategy of Warren Buffett, who has built his wealth through a combination of long-term investing and compound interest. For example, if an investor were to invest $1,000 in a high-yield savings account earning 5% interest, compounded annually, they would earn approximately $1,628 after 10 years, as calculated using the future value formula. In contrast, if they were to invest in a stock index fund with an average annual return of 8%, they could potentially earn around $2,158 over the same period, demonstrating the potential for higher returns through investing in the stock market. Boeing produces ~800 aircraft annually (Boeing annual report), and investors in the company's stock have benefited from the power of compound interest over the long term.
