Example of Roth Ira

Last updated: February 2026

Definition

Roth Individual Retirement Account (Roth IRA) is a type of retirement savings account introduced by Senator William Roth in 1997, allowing individuals to contribute after-tax dollars, which can then grow tax-free and be withdrawn tax-free in retirement.

How It Works

The Roth IRA operates under a distinct set of rules compared to traditional IRAs. Contributions to a Roth IRA are made with after-tax dollars, meaning the contributor has already paid income tax on the money. As a result, the funds in a Roth IRA grow tax-free, and qualified withdrawals are also tax-free. The Internal Revenue Service (IRS) sets annual contribution limits, which were $6,000 in 2022, with an additional $1,000 catch-up contribution allowed for individuals 50 years or older (IRS Publication 590-A). This structure incentivizes saving for retirement by providing tax benefits at the withdrawal stage, rather than at the contribution stage like traditional IRAs.

The mechanism of tax-free growth and withdrawal is crucial for the appeal of Roth IRAs. Since the contributions are made with after-tax dollars, the tax burden is absorbed at the time of contribution, allowing the entire amount, including earnings, to be withdrawn without additional taxes in retirement, provided certain conditions are met. One of these conditions is the five-year rule, which states that the account must have been open for at least five years to qualify for tax-free and penalty-free withdrawals of earnings. This rule encourages long-term savings and planning.

Roth IRAs also offer flexibility in terms of Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs). Unlike traditional IRAs, which require individuals to take RMDs starting at age 72, Roth IRAs do not have RMDs during the owner's lifetime. This means that individuals can keep their Roth IRA savings intact for as long as they live, allowing them to grow tax-free without mandatory withdrawals. This flexibility, combined with the tax-free growth and withdrawal features, makes Roth IRAs an attractive option for estate planning, as they can be passed to beneficiaries without the burden of income tax.

Key Components

  • Contribution limits: The annual limits set by the IRS on how much can be contributed to a Roth IRA, which can affect the amount one can save each year for retirement.
  • Income eligibility: The income limits that determine who is eligible to contribute to a Roth IRA, which can change annually and affect the ability to contribute.
  • Tax-free growth: The feature that allows the funds in a Roth IRA to grow without being subject to taxes, increasing the savings over time.
  • Five-year rule: The requirement that the account must be at least five years old to withdraw earnings tax-free and penalty-free, encouraging long-term investment.
  • No RMDs: The absence of required minimum distributions during the owner's lifetime, providing flexibility in retirement planning and estate planning.
  • Beneficiary rules: The regulations governing how Roth IRA assets are distributed to beneficiaries, which can impact estate planning strategies.

Common Misconceptions

  • Myth: Roth IRAs are only for low-income individuals. Fact: While income limits apply to who can contribute to a Roth IRA, they are not exclusively for low-income individuals; the limits are set based on filing status and income level (IRS Publication 590-A).
  • Myth: All Roth IRA withdrawals are tax-free. Fact: Only qualified withdrawals, which meet certain conditions such as the five-year rule and being 59 1/2 or older, are tax-free; non-qualified withdrawals may be subject to taxes and penalties.
  • Myth: Roth IRAs have required minimum distributions like traditional IRAs. Fact: Roth IRAs do not have RMDs during the owner's lifetime, offering more flexibility in retirement planning.
  • Myth: You can contribute to a Roth IRA at any age. Fact: While there is no age limit for contributing to a Roth IRA, the ability to contribute is based on earned income and income limits, not age (IRS Publication 590-A).

In Practice

Consider the example of Jane, a 35-year-old software engineer with an annual income of $120,000. She decides to contribute $6,000 to a Roth IRA each year, starting from 2022, until she reaches age 65, assuming she remains eligible based on her income. Over this 30-year period, her contributions total $180,000. Assuming an average annual return of 7%, her Roth IRA could grow to approximately $530,000 by the time she reaches 65, based on compound interest calculations. Since she has met the five-year rule and is over 59 1/2, Jane can withdraw from her Roth IRA tax-free and penalty-free, providing a significant source of retirement income.
