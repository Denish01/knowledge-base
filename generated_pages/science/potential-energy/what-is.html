What Is Potential Energy?

Last updated: February 2026

Potential energy is the stored energy an object has due to its position, configuration, or state, which can be converted into kinetic energy when the object is put into motion.

Potential energy is a fundamental concept in physics that explains how objects can store energy without visibly moving. This type of energy is often associated with an object's height, stretch, or compression, and it has the potential to be converted into kinetic energy, which is the energy of motion. For example, a ball at the top of a hill has potential energy due to its height, and as it rolls down the hill, this potential energy is converted into kinetic energy.

The concept of potential energy can be applied to various situations, including objects that are stretched or compressed, such as springs or rubber bands. When an object is stretched or compressed, it stores energy, which can be released when the object returns to its original shape. This stored energy is a form of potential energy, and it can be used to perform work when the object is released. Additionally, potential energy can be associated with the configuration of an object, such as a block of wood on a inclined plane. The block of wood has potential energy due to its position on the inclined plane, and as it slides down the plane, this potential energy is converted into kinetic energy.

The concept of potential energy is closely related to the concept of work and energy. When an object is moved from a lower position to a higher position, work is done on the object, and the object gains potential energy. Conversely, when an object is moved from a higher position to a lower position, the object loses potential energy, and this energy is converted into kinetic energy. This relationship between work and energy is a fundamental principle in physics, and it is essential for understanding how objects move and interact with their environment.

The key components of potential energy include:

  • Position: The height or location of an object in a gravitational field or other external field
  • Configuration: The arrangement or shape of an object, such as a stretched or compressed spring
  • State: The condition of an object, such as its temperature or phase
  • Force: The external force applied to an object, such as gravity or friction
  • Distance: The distance over which an object is moved or displaced
  • Mass: The amount of matter in an object, which affects its potential energy

Common misconceptions about potential energy include:

  • Believing that potential energy is only associated with objects that are moving, when in fact it is associated with objects that are at rest but have the potential to move
  • Thinking that potential energy is a type of kinetic energy, when in fact it is a separate type of energy that can be converted into kinetic energy
  • Assuming that potential energy is only relevant in situations involving gravity, when in fact it can be applied to a wide range of situations, including objects that are stretched or compressed
  • Believing that potential energy is a fixed or constant quantity, when in fact it can change depending on the position, configuration, or state of an object

A real-world example of potential energy is a roller coaster at the top of a hill. The roller coaster has potential energy due to its height, and as it rolls down the hill, this potential energy is converted into kinetic energy. As the roller coaster moves down the hill, its potential energy decreases, and its kinetic energy increases. This conversion of potential energy into kinetic energy is what allows the roller coaster to move and accelerate down the hill.

Summary: Potential energy is the stored energy an object has due to its position, configuration, or state, which can be converted into kinetic energy when the object is put into motion, and it plays a fundamental role in understanding how objects move and interact with their environment.
