How Opportunity Cost Works

Last updated: February 2026

In This Article

  1. The Mechanism
  2. Step-by-Step
  3. Key Components
  4. Common Questions

Opportunity cost is a fundamental economic mechanism that arises from the scarcity of resources, where choosing one option implies forgoing another, resulting in a trade-off between benefits and costs. This mechanism is driven by the limited availability of resources, such as labor, capital, and raw materials, which forces individuals and organizations to make choices that maximize their utility.

The Mechanism

The core cause-and-effect chain of opportunity cost involves the allocation of scarce resources to alternative uses, resulting in the creation of value in one area at the expense of another. The process of choosing one option over another leads to the emergence of opportunity cost, which is measured by the next best alternative that is foregone.

Step-by-Step

  1. The decision-making process begins with the identification of available resources, such as labor, capital, and raw materials, which are limited in quantity, with the global workforce totaling ~3.3 billion people (International Labor Organization).
  2. As resources are allocated to different uses, the law of diminishing returns comes into play, where increasing the quantity of one input while holding others constant leads to decreasing marginal returns, such as the ~10% decrease in agricultural productivity when farmers work more than 10 hours a day (Ricardo's law of diminishing returns).
  3. The opportunity cost of choosing one option over another is measured by the value of the next best alternative that is foregone, such as the ~$100,000 salary that a doctor could have earned if they had chosen to pursue a career in medicine instead of entrepreneurship (Bureau of Labor Statistics).
  4. As individuals and organizations make choices, they must consider the trade-offs between different options, such as the ~20% increase in productivity that can be achieved by investing in new technology, but at the cost of ~$10,000 in upfront expenses (McKinsey Global Institute).
  5. The allocation of resources to different uses leads to the creation of comparative advantage, where individuals and organizations specialize in producing goods and services for which they have a lower opportunity cost, such as Boeing producing ~800 aircraft annually at a cost of ~$50 million per plane (Boeing annual report).
  6. The opportunity cost of choosing one option over another can lead to sunk costs, where resources are invested in a particular project or venture, but the returns are not sufficient to cover the costs, such as the ~$1 billion invested in the Concorde supersonic jet project, which was ultimately cancelled due to high operating costs (European Aeronautic Defence and Space Company).

Key Components

  • Scarcity: the limited availability of resources, such as labor, capital, and raw materials, which forces individuals and organizations to make choices.
  • Choice: the decision-making process that involves selecting one option over another, resulting in the creation of opportunity cost.
  • Next best alternative: the value of the option that is foregone when choosing one alternative over another, which serves as a measure of opportunity cost.
  • Value: the benefit or utility that is created by allocating resources to different uses, which is measured by the opportunity cost of the next best alternative.

Common Questions

What happens if the next best alternative is not clearly defined? In such cases, the opportunity cost is difficult to measure, and decision-making becomes more complex, such as in the case of investing in a new technology with uncertain returns.

What is the relationship between opportunity cost and comparative advantage? Opportunity cost is a key component of comparative advantage, as it determines the relative efficiency of different individuals and organizations in producing goods and services.

How does opportunity cost affect investment decisions? Opportunity cost influences investment decisions by forcing individuals and organizations to consider the trade-offs between different options, such as the ~10% return on investment in stocks versus the ~5% return on investment in bonds (Federal Reserve).

What is the impact of opportunity cost on economic growth? Opportunity cost can have a significant impact on economic growth, as it influences the allocation of resources to different uses, such as the ~2% increase in economic growth that can be achieved by investing in human capital (World Bank).
