How Pythagorean Theorem Works

1. QUICK ANSWER: The Pythagorean theorem is a mathematical concept that works by calculating the length of the hypotenuse of a right-angled triangle using the lengths of the other two sides. This is achieved through a simple equation that relates the lengths of the sides, allowing for the determination of the hypotenuse.

2. STEP-BY-STEP PROCESS: To understand how the Pythagorean theorem works, it is helpful to break down the process into steps. First, identify a right-angled triangle, which is a triangle with one angle that is 90 degrees. Then, label the two sides that form the right angle as the base and height, and the side opposite the right angle as the hypotenuse. Next, measure the lengths of the base and height, which can be any unit of length such as inches or meters. After that, square the lengths of the base and height, which means multiplying each length by itself. Finally, add the squared lengths of the base and height together, and then take the square root of the result to find the length of the hypotenuse.

3. KEY COMPONENTS: The key components involved in the Pythagorean theorem are the base, height, and hypotenuse of a right-angled triangle. The base and height are the two sides that form the right angle, and their lengths are used to calculate the length of the hypotenuse. The hypotenuse is the side opposite the right angle, and its length is the result of the calculation. The equation a^2 + b^2 = c^2 is also a crucial component, where a and b are the lengths of the base and height, and c is the length of the hypotenuse.

The ladder forms the hypotenuse of a right-angled triangle, while the wall and the ground form the base and height. This analogy makes it intuitive to understand how the lengths of the sides of a right-angled triangle are related.
