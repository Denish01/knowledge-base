Sanitation Compared is a framework for evaluating the effectiveness of different sanitation systems, considering factors such as cost, environmental impact, and public health outcomes, developed by the World Health Organization (WHO).

Definition

Sanitation Compared is a systematic approach to assessing sanitation systems, originating from the work of Engineer Joseph Bazalgette, who designed the first modern sewer system in London in 1858.

How It Works

The Sanitation Compared framework involves a comprehensive analysis of a sanitation system's technical, financial, and social aspects, using Ricardo's comparative advantage model to evaluate the efficiency of different systems. This approach considers the total cost of ownership, including capital expenditures, operating costs, and maintenance expenses, as well as the environmental impact, such as greenhouse gas emissions and water pollution, with the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) estimating that inadequate sanitation costs the global economy ~$260 billion annually. The framework also assesses the public health outcomes, including the incidence of waterborne diseases, such as cholera and diarrhea, which affect ~1.6 million people worldwide each year, according to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC).

  • Myth: Sanitation systems are a luxury that only wealthy countries can afford — Fact: The World Bank estimates that every dollar invested in sanitation generates a return of ~$5 in economic benefits, making sanitation a sound investment for countries at all income levels.
  • Myth: Sanitation systems are only relevant in urban areas — Fact: The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) estimates that ~70% of the global population lives in rural areas, where sanitation systems are often inadequate or non-existent, highlighting the need for rural sanitation solutions.
  • Myth: Sanitation systems are solely the responsibility of government agencies — Fact: The private sector plays a significant role in providing sanitation services, with companies such as Veolia and SUEZ operating sanitation systems in many countries.
  • Myth: Sanitation systems are a fixed infrastructure that cannot be changed — Fact: The city of Copenhagen has implemented a district heating system that uses wastewater to generate heat, reducing greenhouse gas emissions by ~70%, demonstrating the potential for innovative sanitation solutions.
