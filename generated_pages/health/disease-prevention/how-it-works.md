# How Disease Prevention Works

Disease prevention works through a complex mechanism involving **immunization**, **hygiene**, and **disease surveillance**, which ultimately reduces the incidence of infectious diseases by interrupting the transmission of pathogens.

## The Mechanism
The core cause-and-effect chain of disease prevention involves the reduction of **pathogen** transmission through **vaccination**, which induces **herd immunity** and prevents the spread of diseases. This process is facilitated by **public health infrastructure**, including **laboratories** and **hospitals**, which provide the necessary resources for disease surveillance and response.

## Step-by-1
1. **Vaccine development**: The process of creating a vaccine involves identifying a specific **pathogen**, such as the influenza virus, and developing a vaccine that can induce an **immune response** against it, resulting in a **90%** reduction in disease incidence (Ricardo's comparative advantage model).
2. **Vaccination campaigns**: Organized vaccination campaigns, such as those conducted by the **World Health Organization**, can vaccinate up to **80%** of a target population, resulting in a significant reduction in disease transmission.
3. **Disease surveillance**: Continuous monitoring of disease incidence and outbreaks, using tools such as **GIS mapping**, allows for the early detection of disease transmission and enables targeted interventions, resulting in a **40%** reduction in disease-related mortality.
4. **Contact tracing**: Identifying and isolating individuals who have come into contact with an infected person can reduce the transmission of diseases by up to **60%**, as seen in the **Ebola outbreak** in West Africa.
5. **Hygiene practices**: Promoting **hand hygiene** and **respiratory etiquette** can reduce the transmission of diseases by up to **50%**, as demonstrated by the **Centers for Disease Control and Prevention**.
6. **Public health infrastructure**: Investing in **public health infrastructure**, such as **laboratories** and **hospitals**, can improve disease response times by up to **30%**, resulting in better health outcomes.

## Key Components
- **Vaccines**: Provide **immunity** against specific pathogens, reducing disease incidence and transmission.
- **Public health infrastructure**: Enables disease surveillance, response, and prevention, and provides the necessary resources for vaccination campaigns.
- **Disease surveillance**: Allows for the early detection of disease transmission and enables targeted interventions.
- **Hygiene practices**: Reduce the transmission of diseases by preventing the spread of pathogens.

## Common Questions
What happens if a vaccination campaign fails to achieve **herd immunity**? If a vaccination campaign fails to achieve **herd immunity**, the disease can continue to spread, resulting in a significant increase in disease incidence and transmission.
